What's the Difference?

Margin and markup are both measures of profitability, but they're calculated differently and often confused. Using the wrong one can lead to pricing mistakes that eat into your profits.

Aspect Margin Markup
Based on Selling Price Cost Price
Formula (Price - Cost) / Price (Price - Cost) / Cost
Example: $50 cost, $80 price 37.5% 60%
Used for Financial analysis Pricing decisions

The Formulas

Margin % = (Selling Price - Cost) / Selling Price × 100 Markup % = (Selling Price - Cost) / Cost × 100 Selling Price = Cost / (1 - Margin%)

Example Calculation

Scenario: Product Pricing

Cost (COGS): $50

Selling Price: $80

Profit: $30

Margin: $30 / $80 = 37.5%

Markup: $30 / $50 = 60%

Notice: Same profit, different percentages! This is why understanding the difference matters.

Why This Matters

  • Avoid Pricing Mistakes: If you aim for a 40% margin but use markup math, you'll underprice and lose profit.
  • Better Financial Planning: Margin is what shows up on P&L statements, not markup.
  • Platform Fee Calculations: Amazon, Shopify, and payment processors take percentages of selling price (margin-based), not cost.
  • Investor Communication: Investors and acquirers think in margins, not markup.

Common Mistake to Avoid

The 50% Markup Trap

Many sellers think: "My cost is $50, I'll add 50% markup = $75 price."

But 50% markup ≠ 50% margin!

At $75 price:

  • Markup = 50% ✓
  • Margin = ($75-$50)/$75 = 33.3% ✗

To get 50% margin: Price = $50 / (1-0.5) = $100
