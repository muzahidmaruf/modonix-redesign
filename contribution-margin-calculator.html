What is Contribution Margin?

Contribution margin measures the incremental profit earned for each unit sold, after subtracting variable costs. It shows how much revenue is available to cover fixed costs and contribute to profit.

The Formulas

Contribution Margin ($) = Revenue – Variable Costs Contribution Margin Ratio (%) = (Revenue – Variable Costs) ÷ Revenue × 100

Example Calculation

Per-Unit Example

Sale Price per Unit: $100

Variable Cost per Unit: $60

Contribution Margin: $100 – $60 = $40 per unit

CM Ratio: $40 ÷ $100 = 40%

Each unit sold contributes $40 toward covering fixed costs and profit.

Break-Even Analysis

Contribution margin is essential for calculating your break-even point:

Break-Even Units = Fixed Costs ÷ CM per Unit Break-Even Revenue = Fixed Costs ÷ CM Ratio

Break-Even Example

Monthly Fixed Costs: $20,000

CM per Unit: $40

Break-Even: $20,000 ÷ $40 = 500 units/month

Why Contribution Margin Matters

  • Pricing Decisions: Helps determine minimum viable prices
  • Product Mix: Identify which products contribute most to profitability
  • Scale Decisions: Understand the profit impact of increased volume
  • Cost Control: Focus on reducing variable costs that directly impact margin

Contribution Margin vs Gross Margin

Unlike gross margin, contribution margin excludes fixed production costs (like factory overhead). It focuses purely on costs that vary with each unit sold—making it more useful for short-term decision making.

Interpreting Your Results

  • High CM (50%+): Strong profitability per unit. Room for discounts or increased marketing spend.
  • Moderate CM (30-50%): Healthy but monitor variable costs closely.
  • Low CM (<30%): Thin margins. Consider price increases or cost reduction strategies.
  • Negative CM: Losing money on each sale. Immediate action required.
